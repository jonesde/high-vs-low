## Overview

**Feed the Flock** is a pastoral religious address (likely delivered by a Latter-day Saint General Authority) that uses personal anecdotes, scriptural references, and chaplain reports to exhort church leaders and families to actively care for young people serving in the military. The speaker emphasizes the emotional toll of isolation, criticizes institutional neglect, and challenges the audience to replace passive ritual with tangible affection, letters, and consistent communication. 

The text is overwhelmingly **High Law aligned** in its normative stances. While it employs religious vocabulary (prophets, sin, devil, divine command), the underlying principles championed are relational, voluntary, and centered on compassion, mutual care, and the protection of the vulnerable. The author consistently frames leadership and faith as active stewardship rather than coercive control, explicitly rejecting empty ritual in favor of practical love. The dominant framework is High Law, with religious language functioning as a vehicle for relational ethics rather than a mechanism for legalistic obedience.
